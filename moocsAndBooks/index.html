In short

In France, a wide majority of engineers spend the first two years of their higher education preparing for selective entrance examinations to the "Grandes Ã©coles", that is the french graduate schools. These two years involve a lot of maths and physics, but also chemistry, philosophy, biology. It is great in the sense that it gives a wide scientific culture. But it means two years with almost no Computer Science, which leaves only 3 years to learn a lot of content before graduating with a Master's degree. As as result, I have been frustrated by the fact that some very useful things were not tought in my school. To cope with that, I started reading technical books, and taking Massive Open Online Courses.

MOOCs

MOOCs are great, because they are often self-paced, or at least flexible. Over the last year, I have taken 5 of them without counting some that I have not finished yet. Most of them are related with cutting-edge / trending technologies that I wanted to add to my skill set as they are quite popular. Below is a list of the MOOCs I have taken or that I am taking, and a personnal rate :

  • Machine Learning by Andrew Ng (Stanford University) on Coursera. 5/5, I highly recommend it to anyone willing to get into ML. Certificate.
  • Introduction to Apache Spark by A.D. Joseph (UC Berkeley) on edX. 4/5, pretty good to get started with Spark. Certificate.
  • Big data analysis with Apache Spark by A.D. Joseph (UC Berkeley) on edX. 4/5, had really good notebooks providing much needed hands-on practice. Certificate.
  • Distributed Machine Learning with Apache Spark by A.D. Joseph (UC Berkeley) on edX. 3/5, a lot of redundancy with the previous while they were part of the same XSeries, that is a serie of course to develop deep knowledge in a subject. Certificate.
  • Visual Perception and the Brain by Dale Purves (Duke University) on Coursera. 4/5, quite hard as Life Sciences are definitely not my specialty, but I found it to be a very good food for thoughts when getting into Computer Vision.
  • Introduction to Computer Vision by Aaron Bobick (Georgia Tech) on Udacity. In progress, really enjoying it until now.
  • Deep Learning by Google on Udacity. In progress, pretty good until now, really hands on (using TensorFlow).

Books

MOOCs are very useful, but I feel like when you want to really get deep into a subject, reading THE book about it is the the best way. It does take a lot more time, as solid reference books are usually worth a few hundreds hours of lecture if you take the time to really understand what they teach, but I think it is worth it! I am currently reading, slowly but steadily:

  • Peter Norvig's "Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach"
  • Ian Goodfellow's "Deep Learning". You can find it online for free here.
  • Raja Parasuraman's "Neuroergonomics: The Brain at Work". Brace yourself, this one is a tough read!
